The UK government is considering using "methane blockers" to reduce emissions of greenhouse gases from cows as part of its net-zero climate goal.

Methane produced by cattle through burps and manure is a significant contributor to greenhouse gas emissions, with cows and other farm animals accounting for about 14% of human-induced climate emissions.

A consultation launched in August examined how new types of animal feed could be used to reduce digestive emissions. High-efficacy methane-suppressing products are expected to enter the market in 2025, and farmers could be forced to use them if they prove effective.

2. Tobacco companies to be billed for cleaning up cigarette butts in Spain

TTobacco companies will be required to pay for the cleaning of discarded cigarette ends in Spain under new environmental regulations.

The legislation came into effect in January, is part of a wider package that includes a ban on single-use plastic cutlery and plates, cotton buds, expanded polystyrene cups and plastic straws, as well as a reduction in plastic food packaging.

The measures comply with a European Union directive that aims to restrict the use of single-use plastics and require polluters to clean up their waste.

The tobacco companies will also be responsible for educating the public about the importance of not littering with cigarette butts. It is unclear how the clean-up will be funded or implemented. 3. Building a Sustainable Future with LEGO Bricks

LEGO is implementing environmental initiatives to create a more sustainable future.

Its Replay program enables customers to donate used bricks to children in need, including mixed sets or missing pieces. The program has donated 335,000 kg of LEGO bricks in Canada alone, which are enjoyed by over 224,000 children.

The company also aims to produce bricks from more sustainable materials, such as PET plastic bottles and sugar cane-derived plastic. Its plant-based material has been used to create around 150 elements including trees, bushes and LEGO minifigure accessories.

Additionally, LEGO is committed to reducing packaging waste, aiming to use sustainably sourced materials that are renewable or certified recycled by 2025. The company is also replacing single-use plastic bags with paper bags made from Forest Stewardship Council-certified forests.
