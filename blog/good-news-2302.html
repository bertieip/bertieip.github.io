1. Ocean treaty: Historic agreement reached after decade of talks

After 10 years of negotiations, nations have agreed to The High Seas Treaty, which aims to protect and recuperate marine nature by placing 30% of the seas into protected areas by 2030.

The treaty will put limits on fishing, shipping lanes, and deep-sea mining activities in the new protected areas. This marks the first international agreement on ocean protection since the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea in 1982.

The treaty is expected to protect marine life from overfishing, shipping traffic, and climate change while safeguarding the lives and livelihoods of billions of people. Environmental campaigners have praised the treaty as a historic day for conservation and a sign that protecting nature and people can triumph over geopolitics.

3. Elephants are Doing Their Part to Slow Climate Change

The world's largest carbon sinks, including the ocean, soil, and forests, absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, with forests playing a crucial role in regulating temperature.

However, deforestation for livestock farmland and development projects is causing a decline in forests and their ability to absorb carbon, leading to rising temperatures and climate change.

Studies have shown that elephants in African forests play a vital role in shaping their environments by consuming plants and spreading seeds in their dung. They strip bark from trees and dig up roots, competing with larger trees for light and nutrients, leading to an increase in tree diameter and total above-ground biomass.

By eating the leaves of faster-growing trees and preferring fruit from slower-growing trees, they make room for carbon-rich trees and help spread their seeds through the forest, thus contributing to carbon capture.

Protecting elephants from illegal hunting and trade can protect the forests, reduce carbon in the atmosphere, and slow the effects of climate change.
